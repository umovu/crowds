
# Street Trading, Permits, and Foreign Nationals in South African Metros
## Simulation Briefing — Policy Stress-Test Document

---

## SOURCE: Parliamentary Data (Cogta, 2024)

South Africa's five largest metropolitan municipalities issued over 32,000 street trading
permits across five years. The data was obtained from the Department of Cooperative Governance
and Traditional Affairs (Cogta), responding to a parliamentary question by MK Party member
Mokgaetji Mafagane (National Assembly).

Street trading permit distribution by metro (5-year cumulative):
- Ekurhuleni: 16,567 permits issued
- eThekwini (Durban): 11,700 permits issued
- Tshwane (Pretoria): 2,506 permits issued
- Cape Town: 1,372 permits issued
- City of Johannesburg: DID NOT RESPOND despite parliamentary follow-ups

Cogta Minister Velenkosini Hlabisa acknowledged there is NO uniform national methodology
for measuring informal versus formal trading. Each municipality operates independent permit
systems under its own bylaws. No centralized national database exists.

CBD informal vs formal trading composition:
- eThekwini: informal trading dominates at 55.2% of CBD activity; formal 44.8%
- Tshwane: formal businesses lead at 67.2%; informal trading 32.8%
- Ekurhuleni: formal businesses 78.9%; informal trading 21.1%
- Cape Town: cannot provide percentages — "free trade areas" operate without centralized tracking

Johannesburg's silence on permit data is significant: the city has the highest concentration
of foreign national informal traders and the most contested street trading environment in SA,
yet it has provided no official figures to Parliament.

## KEY ACTORS

### South African Informal Traders (Local Vendors)
Predominantly Black South African women and men selling food, clothing, airtime, and
household goods from designated street stalls or undesignated spots. Many hold permits
issued by their metro; many do not. Organised under bodies like the South African Informal
Traders Alliance (SAITA) and various city-level trader associations.
Key grievances: foreign nationals occupy their permitted spots, undercut their prices,
operate without permits, and receive police protection through bribes. Many have been
trading the same spot for 10-20 years.
Province: Gauteng, KwaZulu-Natal, Western Cape
Income: R2,000-R6,000/month depending on location and goods

### Foreign National Street Traders (Undocumented / Asylum Seekers)
Traders from Zimbabwe, Mozambique, Ethiopia, Somalia, Nigeria, and DRC operating in
Johannesburg CBD, eThekwini beachfront, Tshwane Church Street, and Cape Town Grand Parade.
Many hold Section 22 asylum seeker permits that do not explicitly authorize trading.
Many entered through Beitbridge, Lebombo, or unmonitored border points.
Operate longer hours (6am-9pm), buy collectively through importing networks, sell below
local prices. Targeted during July 2021 looting (spaza shops burned), Operation Dudula
raids (2022-2023), and periodic JMPD/SAPS confiscation operations.
Lawyers for Human Rights (LHR) estimates 70-80% of foreign national traders in Joburg
operate in a legal grey zone — not explicitly illegal, not formally authorized.

### Operation Dudula Movement
Founded by Nhlanhla "Lux" Dlamini in 2021 following the July unrest. Conducts
door-to-door and market raids to identify undocumented foreign nationals and report
them to Home Affairs and SAPS for deportation. Claims 50,000+ members in Gauteng.
Argues it represents unemployed South African youth who cannot compete with foreign
nationals. Classified by civil society as a xenophobic vigilante movement; classified
by supporters as a legitimate community protection response to state failure.
Nhlanhla Lux was arrested in 2022 for housebreaking charges unrelated to Dudula.
Movement continued under leadership of Zandile Mafe and others.
Has clashed physically with foreign national traders at Jeppestown, Yeoville, and
Alexandra markets. Police response to Dudula raids described as "ambiguous" — sometimes
intervening, sometimes standing by.

### City of Johannesburg (CoJ) Metropolitan Police Department (JMPD)
Responsible for enforcing street trading bylaws including confiscation of unlicensed goods,
removal of unlawful traders, and permit verification. Conducts periodic "clean-up operations"
in the Joburg CBD.
Accused of systematic corruption: foreign national traders report paying R200-R500 per week
to JMPD officers to avoid confiscation. SA vendors report JMPD selectively targets them
while ignoring foreign nationals who have paid.
CoJ did not respond to parliamentary requests for permit data — the only major metro to
refuse disclosure. City spokesperson did not provide explanation.

### Department of Home Affairs
Processes asylum applications and issues refugee documentation. Has a backlog of over
180,000 pending asylum applications (2023 figure). Many foreign nationals have been
waiting 5-10 years for refugee status determination while holding temporary Section 22
permits. During this period, their right to work and trade is legally unclear.
Runs Lindela Repatriation Centre (Krugersdorp) where undocumented migrants are held
pending deportation. Lindela has been criticised for overcrowding and inhumane conditions.
Home Affairs officials have been arrested for selling fraudulent permits and ID documents
to foreign nationals.

### Lawyers for Human Rights (LHR) and Civil Society
NGO providing free legal representation to asylum seekers, refugees, and stateless persons.
Successfully challenged JMPD confiscation operations as unlawful. Argues that xenophobic
raids violate constitutional rights to dignity and equality regardless of documentation status.
Seen by South African vendors and Operation Dudula as protecting illegal immigrants at the
expense of citizens. LHR argues the state's failure to process asylum applications rapidly
creates the undocumented population that others then exploit politically.

### MK Party (uMkhonto we Sizwe Party)
Zuma-aligned political party. Member Mokgaetji Mafagane raised the street trading permit
question in Parliament, signalling the party's intent to position itself as a champion of
South African workers against foreign economic competition. Strong base in KwaZulu-Natal
and parts of Gauteng. Populist messaging targets "economic refugees stealing SA jobs."

### Cogta (Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs)
Minister Velenkosini Hlabisa (IFP). Responsible for overseeing municipal governance.
Acknowledged in parliamentary response that there is no national framework for street
trading regulation — each metro makes its own rules. This fragmentation means foreign
nationals can exploit regulatory gaps between municipalities.

### eThekwini (Durban) Informal Traders
Durban has the highest proportion of informal trading in its CBD at 55.2%. Strong
presence of Zulu-speaking SA vendors as well as Somali, Nigerian, and Ethiopian traders.
History of violent conflict at Warwick Junction market — one of Africa's largest informal
trading hubs. eThekwini issued the second-highest number of permits (11,700) but
enforcement of permit conditions is weak.

### Ekurhuleni Informal Traders
Ekurhuleni (East Rand) issued the most permits nationally (16,567) — more than all other
metros combined. Covers areas like Germiston, Benoni, Boksburg, Springs. High concentration
of Mozambican and Zimbabwean traders at taxi ranks and railway stations.

### SASSA Grant Recipients Who Supplement with Trading
Many South African traders combine SASSA social relief of distress (SRD) grants (R370/month)
with informal trading income. The grant is insufficient alone. Trading supplements survival.
Foreign nationals cannot access SASSA grants — driving them to work longer hours for less.

## KEY TENSIONS FOR SIMULATION

1. PERMIT VACUUM: Joburg — the most contested city — has no public permit data and
   no coherent policy. This vacuum is exploited by foreign nationals (who can't get permits
   anyway) and by JMPD officers (who collect informal "fees").

2. PRICE UNDERCUTTING: Foreign traders buy collectively and sell below local prices.
   SA vendors cannot compete without access to the same import networks. This is experienced
   as unfair competition but is not illegal.

3. DOCUMENTATION GREY ZONE: Section 22 asylum permits do not explicitly authorize or
   prohibit trading. Home Affairs' backlog keeps thousands in this grey zone for years.
   No permit = no protection = prey to corruption and raids.

4. CORRUPTION AS INFORMAL REGULATION: JMPD corruption effectively creates an informal
   permit system where foreign traders pay for the right to operate. This excludes
   poorer SA vendors who cannot or will not pay.

5. POLITICAL MOBILISATION: MK Party, EFF, and Operation Dudula all exploit the
   street vendor issue to mobilise unemployed South African youth against a visible,
   vulnerable scapegoat. Foreign traders become proxy targets for systemic failure.

6. VIOLENCE THRESHOLD: The distance between "legitimate protest" and physical attack
   on foreign-owned stalls has been short in SA history (July 2021, 2019 xenophobia
   attacks, 2008 Alexandrea attacks). The simulation should test what tips ordinary
   frustration into crowd violence.

7. FORMAL VS INFORMAL ECONOMY: Many SA vendors resist formalisation because permit
   fees, designated zones, and trading hours reduce income flexibility. The informal
   economy absorbs what the formal economy cannot — both for SA citizens and migrants.
